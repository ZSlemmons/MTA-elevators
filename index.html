How accessible is the NYC Subway?

Ask any average New Yorker and they’ll tell you that the subway is the life blood of the city. With 8.5 million people sharing a meager 300 square meters of land, public transit is a necessity. If you’ve ever tried to take a cab during rush hour, you’ll understand why.

Free from the whims of city traffic, the subway is an efficient and cost effective way to get around. For $2.90 you can get almost anywhere you want to go within the five boroughs – as long as it’s not on Staten Island.

THE STATIONS

The vast network of interconnected train lines can be accessed through 475 subway stations citywide

THE PROBLEM

But many of these stations are inaccessible to the estimated 11% of New Yorkers with ambulatory disabilities.

Analysis shows that only about one third of MTA stations are wheelchair accessible. The lack of accessibility can be attributed in large part to the subway’s age. The oldest parts of the system first opened in 1904, with construction continuing to expand the network through the 1920s.

Compared with other U.S. metro systems, the MTA is lagging behind in terms of accessibility. Washington D.C.’s metro system is fully accessible. Chicago’s “L” system is 70% accessible according to the Chicago Transit Authority website.

However, these comparisons don’t take into account factors like the NYC subway’s age and size. The first sections of the D.C. metro system didn’t open until 1976, and while Chicago’s “L” is even older than the subway, it is also much smaller – with only about one third the number of stations.

A more accurate comparison can be made to some international metro systems. According to its website, London’s Underground, which dates back to the late 19th century, offers step-free access in about one third of its stations – a number very comparable to NYC. The Tokyo metro, which opened in 1927, boasts a whopping 95% accessibility according to the Japan Accessible Tourism Center. The Paris metro, on the other hand, has full wheelchair accessibility in just under 10% of stations according to Ile de France Mobilites.

Following the passage of the Americans with Disabilities Act in 1990, the MTA began the process of introducing elevators into 100 of the city’s most important metro hubs. In 2022, the agency settled a class action lawsuit with disabled riders, promising to dedicate a minimum of 8% of each capital budget to accessibility upgrades. Under the terms of the settlement, the subway system is projected to achieve 95% accessibility by 2055.

In the meantime, accessibility remains a challenge for New Yorkers with ambulatory disabilities. An analysis of a hundred randomly selected trips showed that accessible commutes were an average of nine minutes longer than regular commutes.

That analysis, however, included some trips that were naturally between two accessible stations, meaning the accessible commute was identical to the regular one. With those trips removed from, the analysis shows accessible commutes were, on average, almost 20 minutes longer. Many of the accessible commutes, however, involved taking a bus instead of, or in addition to the subway. For most, no bus-less route was available. The buses however, are subject to traffic delays, particularly during peak commute times.

For those fortunate enough to have accessible stations on both ends of their commute, the success of their trip still hinges on one, notoriously fickle piece of equipment – the elevator.

This can be particularly problematic when an elevator goes out of service at one of those accessible stations.

An analysis of data collected over a 3-day period from Aug. 2 to Aug. 5, showed an average of 9% of elevators were out of service per day.

In total, 129 individual elevators were out of service at some point during that period. Roughly a quarter of those outages were due to the Capital Replacement plan – the agency’s ongoing effort to improve accessibility.

About a third of the outages were the result of unplanned repairs or investigations.

According to MTA data, since 2015, close to half of all elevator outages (47%) have been unplanned. These unplanned outages make it difficult for disabled commuters, who may arrive at their destination only to discover they can’t exit the station

The duration of outages varied widely, with some issues getting resolved in a matter of hours, while others took elevators out of service for days. The average turn around time for elevator repairs was approximately 9 days.

Elevator outages can be particularly disruptive for disabled riders in neighborhoods where accessible stations are few and far between.

City wide, the average distance between accessible stations is half a mile, or about 10 city blocks. That’s close to twice the distance between all subway stations – which is roughly six blocks.

But the distance between accessible stations varies greatly across different parts of the city, and may not align with the areas most in need of accessibility

Analysis shows that areas with the highest number of accessible stations, tend to have the lowest populations of New Yorkers with ambulatory disability

While areas with the largest disabled populations are often underserved by accessible stations.

This discrepancy is not limited to accessible stations. The same areas are typically underserved by subway stations in general. However a disproportionate percentage of accessible stations are packed into Manhattan’s central business district – about one in three compared to 1 in 5 subway stations overall.

The MTA says it’s picking up the pace on accessibility improvements. According to its latest capital plan, the agency has achieved ADA compliance in 46 stations since 2020, with 43 still under construction.

The current budget allocates $7.1 billion to upgrade another 60 stations in the coming years. For disabled New Yorkers, the changes can’t come soon enough.
